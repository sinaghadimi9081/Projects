FEATURES

In pursuit of this critical task, we carefully selected specific sensors to equip our CLAWNTLET, each chosen for its unique capabilities and contributions to the success of our mission.

  • State of the Art Sensors
    •

    As we navigate through Boelter Hall, several state of the art sensors will gather and display information on an OLED screen placed on the CLAWNTLET. It includes a Gas Detection Module, Ultrasonic Sensor, NeoPixel Ring, and Temperature/Humidity Sensor.

  • Gas Detection Module: able to read the potentially harmful gasses in a room and relays the information to the CLAWNTLET for the user to see.
  • Ultrasonic Sensor: used to calculate the distance in cm between an object and the CLAWNTLET to be displayed on the OLED screen.
  • NeoPixel Ring: used to warn the user of potentially harmful conditions by lighting up 12 LEDs with specific colors to indicate danger levels.
  • Temperature/Humidity Sensor: able to read the temperature and humidity in a room and relays the information to the CLAWNTLET for the user to see.

  • OLED Screen

    • The CLAWNTLET features a state of the art OLED screen that reads the various sensors located on the CLAWNTLET and displays the information for the user to see.

        ENGINEERING PROBLEMS

        Like the coding example above, designing the gauntlet was not always easy. We encountered a lot of problems in CAD, coding, circuits, and Arduinos.

        • Solution

          • The mindset we adopted to solve most of these problems was making sure each component worked by itself before it was implemented into the gauntlet. This way, each iteration of our design had only one error at a time. Solving the error, we could move on to the next iteration knowing that all previous iterations worked and whatever we were implementing in the current iteration was the issue.

          One example of an engineering problem was the fact that there is only 1 SDA and 1 SCL pin on the ESP32, which caused errors for us because the OLED and Temperature/Humidity Sensor both require it to talk to the ESP32. With the mindset detailed above, we knew that we only needed to solve the SDA and SCL sharing pins. With more research, we found out it was indeed possible to share SDA and SCL between devices and wired the pins to do so on the breadboard.

        CAD ITERATIONS

        Below is the first prototype of our CLAWNTLET. It is a two piece design that snaps together and would require drilling and glue to attach the necessary components. As a result, we created a second design.

        Our second design was in the shape of a cone, but also proved fruitless. The problem with the cone was that it was too weak and could not support the components. It was also ugly.

        We also made a bowling ball with finger holes inside that would shield the hand. This prototype would lead us to our final hand shield.

        • Final CAD Design

          • Our final design took the cylinder within the second design and added a holder for the battery and breadboard to sit in it. It was also a two piece design with a knuckle holder that housed the sensors which was inspired by the bowling ball. In case of an emergency, the design holds two batteries to ensure the CLAWNTLET is never out of commision. It took about 14 hours for both pieces to finish printing using PLA filament. This final design shows the iteration process of CAD in process. We took what worked in previous designs and ommited what didn't. Of course, the CLAWNTLET would not be itself if it did not have a claw! That's why we installed a state of the art claw on the side that doubles as light! In the end, we came out with a design worthy to be called the CLAWNTLET!
